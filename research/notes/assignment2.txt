Part 1: Deep Research Discovery
Begin with a deep research agent to map the landscape of your chosen topic. This provides orientation before systematic searching.
Choose a focused research question related to your research or dissertation work. The question should be specific enough to yield targeted results but broad enough to surface multiple relevant papers. Good examples:
* "What physics-informed neural network architectures have been applied to turbulent flow modeling?"
* "How have reinforcement learning methods been used for robotic manipulation in unstructured environments?"
* "What computational approaches exist for predicting material fatigue in additively manufactured parts?"
Run a deep research query using one of the major providers (OpenAI Deep Research, Gemini Deep Research, Claude's research mode, or Perplexity Deep Research). Your prompt should request:
1. An overview of the main approaches or methodologies in the area
2. Key research groups or authors working on the problem
3. Current limitations and open challenges
4. Seminal or highly-cited papers to follow up on
Outcome:  (No need to submit this.) Save the complete deep research report and extract a list of at least 10 papers cited in the report that appear most relevant to your question. Note the DOIs or titles for use in subsequent steps.


Part 2: Systematic Expansion with Specialized Tools
Now expand your initial corpus using at least two of the specialized literature tools covered in Class 4. This step ensures you're not relying solely on deep research, which is not systematic.
Tool A: Citation Mapping (ResearchRabbit)
1. Create a ResearchRabbit account if you don't have one (free tier is sufficient)
2. Add 3-5 of the most promising papers from your deep research results as "seed" papers
3. Explore the "Similar Work," "References," and "Citations" views
4. Use the visualization features to understand the citation network
5. Identify 10-15 additional papers that appear in multiple networks or seem highly connected
Tool B: Structured Extraction (Elicit or Consensus)
6. Use your research question (or a refined version) as a query
7. Examine how the tool presents and organizes results
8. For Elicit: explore the structured extraction features (methods, findings, limitations)
9. For Consensus: examine the Consensus Meter if your question can be framed as yes/no
10. Add any new relevant papers to your growing list
Deliverable: Document your experience with each tool. For each tool, note: (a) what you searched, (b) how many results you reviewed, (c) how many papers you added to your list, and (d) one strength and one limitation you observed.
Part 3: Compile and Organize
Consolidate your findings into a manageable corpus for analysis.
1. Gather your papers: Compile a list of approximately 20-30 unique papers from Parts 1 and 2. Remove duplicates.
2. Collect abstracts: Gather the abstracts for all papers. You can export these from your tools or copy them from the paper sources. Create a single document containing all abstracts with clear labels (author, year, title).
3. Obtain full texts where possible: Download PDFs for papers that are freely available or accessible through your institution. Note which papers you could not access.
4. Add to reference manager: Import your papers into Zotero[1] or another reference manager. Create a collection specifically for this assignment.
Outcome: A list of your ~20-30 papers with full citations, organized in your reference manager. Note how many full-text PDFs you were able to obtain.

Part 4: AI-Assisted Prioritization
Use NotebookLM or your Claude/ChatGPT/Gemini project from Assignment 1 to help categorize and prioritize your papers.
Option A: Using NotebookLM
1. Create a new notebook
2. Upload your abstracts document and any full-text PDFs you obtained
3. Ask categorization questions such as:
   * "Group these papers by methodology"
   * "Which papers focus on [specific aspect of your question]?"
   * "Identify papers that address both [concept A] and [concept B]"
4. Ask prioritization questions such as:
   * "Based on these abstracts, which 5 papers seem most foundational to understanding this field?"
   * "Which papers appear to present novel methodologies versus applications of existing methods?"
   * "Which papers most directly address [your specific research question]?"
Option B: Using Your Research Project (Claude/ChatGPT/Gemini)
5. Open the project you created in Assignment 1
6. Upload your abstracts document and key PDFs
7. Ask similar categorization and prioritization questions
8. Ask comparative questions that leverage your existing project context:
   * "How do these new papers relate to the papers already in my project?"
   * "Which of these papers would fill gaps in my current knowledge base?"
Deliverable: Document the questions you asked and the AI's responses. Include at least one categorization and one prioritization question with their results.


Part 5: Create Your Reading Plan
Based on your AI-assisted prioritization, create a structured reading plan.
Tier 1 - To Read Thoroughly (3–5 papers): These are papers to read completely, taking detailed notes. They should be foundational works, methodologically important, or directly relevant to your research question.
Tier 2 - To Read for Methods and Key Findings (5–10 papers): These papers warrant focused reading of the methods section, results, and conclusions, but you may skim background sections.
Tier 3 - To Skim or Set Aside (remaining papers): These papers may be useful for context or as references but don't require immediate deep engagement. Note why each is deprioritized.
Deliverable: A reading plan table (use Google sheets) with columns for: Paper (author, year), Tier (1/2/3), Rationale (why this tier?), and Status (to be updated as you read).

Part 6: Reflection
Write a reflection (400-600 words) addressing the following questions:
1. On discovery tools: How did the results from deep research compare to the specialized tools (ResearchRabbit, Elicit/Consensus)? Did they surface different papers? Which approach felt more comprehensive, and which felt more targeted?
2. On coverage and gaps: Were there papers you expected to find but didn't? Conversely, did any tool surface surprisingly relevant papers you wouldn't have found through traditional searching? What might explain these gaps or discoveries?
3. On AI-assisted prioritization: How useful was the AI's categorization and prioritization? Did you agree with its assessments? Where did you override its suggestions, and why?
4. On the "illusion of understanding": Recall the Messeri & Crockett paper from Class 1, which warns about scientists believing they understand more than they do when using AI. Did this workflow give you a genuine understanding of the literature landscape, or did it create an illusion of completeness? What steps would you take to verify that your literature search is actually comprehensive?
5. On workflow integration: How would you incorporate this workflow (or a modified version) into your actual dissertation research? What would you do differently next time?

Tips for Success
* Start early. Deep research can take 10-30 minutes to complete, and you'll want time to iterate on your question if initial results aren't useful.
* Be specific. A vague research question yields vague results. "Machine learning for engineering" is too broad; "Graph neural networks for predicting composite material failure modes" is appropriately focused.
* Don't trust any single tool. The point of using multiple tools is to surface papers that any individual tool might miss. Look for convergence and divergence.
* Think critically about AI suggestions. The AI doesn't know your specific research goals, your advisor's preferences, or the history of your field. Its prioritization is a starting point, not a final answer.
* Document as you go. It's much easier to write up your experience if you take notes during each step rather than reconstructing afterward.
* This is real work. The reading list you create can become the foundation for an actual literature review section of your dissertation or a survey paper. Invest effort accordingly.


Challenge (Optional)
Throughout this course, we emphasize understanding how AI tools work, not just that they work. Here's an open question for you to investigate:
Does running a deep research query from inside a project (Claude Project, Custom GPT, Gemini Gem) produce different results than running the same query in a fresh conversation?
To explore this:
1. Take the research question you used in Part 1
2. Run the same query (or a very similar one) from inside your Assignment 1 research project
3. Compare the results: Do you get different sources? Different emphasis? Different synthesis?
4. Try this with a second platform if you have access
In your reflection, add a paragraph discussing what you found. Consider:
* Did the project context influence which papers were surfaced, or only how they were presented?
* Does the platform's documentation say anything about how project context affects search?
* What are the implications for how you should structure your research workflows—fresh context for discovery vs. project context for synthesis?
There's no "right answer" here. The goal is to develop your own intuition about when project context helps and when it might introduce bias or narrowing.
